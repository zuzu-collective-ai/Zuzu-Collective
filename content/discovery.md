*Zuzu Collective | Completed via interview, June 2026*

## A1. Your Business

**What you offer:**
Full planning, partial planning, month-of coordination, and bespoke design. Design is the specialty — events that feel *designed*, not decorated. Based in Southern California but works worldwide; actively seeking destination work in Europe (UK, etc.) and Japan.

**What makes you different:**
Deep discovery into what inspires each client, translating that into an immersive, deeply personal event. The goal is not décor — it's an experience where guests feel transported into another world. "Designed rather than decorated." Events feel effortless but are clearly intentional — the je ne sais quoi of a truly considered event.

**Packages and rough price points:**
- Month-of coordination: from ~$2,500
- Partial planning: from ~$4,500
- Full service planning: from ~$8,500
- Design-only: scope-dependent
- Travel and guest count affect all tiers

*Note: Prices recently raised as part of a deliberate shift toward a luxury client base.*

## A2. Your Audiences

### Audience #1 — Current primary (who's booking now)
**Who:** Mid-20s to late 30s, $30–60k budget, planning 9–14 months out. Mix of design-forward clients who want creative direction and logistics-focused clients who just want the day to run smoothly. The latter are not the ideal client.

**Their dream:** Wedding goes exactly as envisioned. No hiccups. Everything comes together.

**Their fears:** The day falling apart; things not matching what they pictured; wasting money.

**Questions they ask:** What do you recommend for design? How do we structure the timeline so the day flows well?

**Where they hang out:** Instagram, Zola, referrals from friends/vendors.

**Strategic note:** This audience is currently over-represented in inquiries. The content strategy should signal a deliberate shift away from DIY/budget positioning toward luxury and design-forward clients.

---

### Audience #2 — Target luxury client (who you want to attract)
**Who:** Mid-20s to late 30s (flexible), budget $80k+, design-savvy, wants an *experience* — not just a wedding. Values creativity, taste, and professionalism above all.

**Their dream:** A fully immersive event. Guests feel like they've stepped into a different world — a book, a film, a place that doesn't exist anywhere else. Moody, saturated, layered. Intentional details that guests notice: patterned linens, pleated glassware, unexpected installations. Nothing generic.

**Their fears:** Hiring someone with bad taste. Getting a venue with white linens, hydrangeas, and a wood dance floor when they wanted something extraordinary.

**The language they use:** "Je ne sais quoi," "vibe," "moody," "immersive," "editorial," "elevated," "experience," "installations." They describe aesthetic feeling, not checklist items.

**Where they hang out:** Instagram (primary), Zola, referrals. Likely also Pinterest for visual inspiration.

**Design aesthetic reference:** Madco (Melissa Andre) — stunning luxury event design, aspirational benchmark for this business.

## A4. Content Types — First Guess

1. **Venue transformations** — before/after reveals showing a space's full potential. Highest instinct for TikTok/Reels. *Challenge: limited transformation footage from past events due to client budget constraints — a gap to close strategically.*
2. **Design advice / expertise content** — "these chairs will elevate your event," explaining why specific details matter, calling out what makes an event look expensive vs. generic.
3. **Installation showcases** — showing immersive design elements in finished events.
4. **Behind-the-scenes process** — planning from a distance (UK event), dry venue logistics (Hawaii), showing the craft of pulling off something difficult.
5. **Destination event storytelling** — Oxford Divinity School ball, Hawaii wedding — the story of how these happened.

*Research will confirm which formats and hooks actually perform. These are starting instincts.*

## Strategic Notes (for research phase)

**The core tension:** This business has luxury *taste* and *vision* but the portfolio doesn't yet fully reflect it (most events were in the $30–60k range). The content strategy must signal luxury expertise *before* the luxury portfolio catches up. This is solvable:

1. Design education content signals taste without needing a big event
2. The Oxford ball is an inherently luxury-signaling story regardless of budget
3. The Hawaii dry-venue logistics story signals professional-grade problem solving
4. Specific detail content (the patterned linen, the pleated glass) can be created as explainers without requiring a new event
5. Collaboration content with high-end photographers/florists builds aspirational association

**Research priority questions:**
- What content formats perform best for luxury wedding planners on TikTok and Instagram Reels right now?
- What hooks stop the scroll for the "bad taste" fear audience?
- How do planners without a full luxury portfolio position toward luxury clients through content?
- What design education content performs in the wedding space?
- Which accounts are winning in this niche and what's their pattern?

## PIECE 01 — "My Wedding Design Icks"

**Format:** TikTok + Instagram Reel (talking head or text-on-screen with BTS footage overlay)
**Platform priority:** TikTok first, Reel second
**Drive type:** Fear (fear of the generic, fear of bad taste)
**Metrics goal:** Shares + Comments
**Proof used:** Planner's design POV / professional opinion

---

### Hook options (film 2–3, test which lands)

**Hook A (primary — label + fear):**
> "If you're planning a wedding and you don't want it to look like every other wedding at your venue — avoid these."

**Hook B (variation — ick format direct):**
> "My wedding design icks. As a designer, these are the things I'd never let a couple do."

**Hook C (experiment — bold contrast):**
> "There's a difference between a wedding that's decorated and a wedding that's designed. Here's how to tell which one you're planning."

---

### Script

**HOOK** *(first 3 seconds — text on screen or spoken directly to camera)*
> "My wedding design icks — things I'd never let a couple do."

**RETAIN** *(the list — be specific, be opinionated, brief on each)*

1. **White linens on round tables.** They're not neutral. They're a default. Defaults read as "we didn't think about this."

2. **Hydrangeas as the main floral.** They're everywhere. If your florist's first suggestion is hydrangeas, ask what else they're thinking.

3. **Wood dance floor in a ballroom.** The contrast almost never works. The floor tells the story of the room. Make sure it's telling the right one.

4. **Matching everything.** Bridesmaids, florals, linens, signage — all the same shade of dusty rose. Cohesion is not the same as matching. One is intentional. The other is a checklist.

5. **The uplighting color.** Purple and blue uplighting in a neutral space. It immediately reads "corporate event." If you're using uplighting, let it enhance — not announce — the room.

6. **Generic chair choice.** The Chiavari chair had a moment. That moment ended. Your chair is in every photo. Choose accordingly.

**BRIDGE** *(turn from list to viewer)*
> "These aren't small details. They're the difference between a wedding that feels designed and one that just feels... decorated. Your guests won't be able to name what's off — but they'll feel it."

**REWARD** *(the takeaway)*
> "A truly designed event doesn't just look beautiful in photos. It feels like somewhere specific. Somewhere you've never been before. That's what I build."

**CTA** *(end card or caption only — never in the body)*
> *Caption:* "If you want your event to feel designed, not just decorated — link in bio."

---

### Production notes
- Talking head works perfectly here — opinion content is more trustworthy from a face
- Can overlay with BTS footage of past events (ideally the Oxford ball or Hawaii) during the list portion
- Text on screen for each "ick" item helps retention and saves on TikTok
- Keep under 60 seconds — aim for 45

## PIECE 02 — "$35k Hawaii Wedding. Dry Venue. We Pulled It Off."

**Format:** TikTok (story format) + Instagram carousel (aftermath/details version)
**Platform priority:** TikTok first
**Drive type:** Fear (fear of budget not being enough) + Desire (proof it's possible)
**Metrics goal:** Watch time + Saves + DM inquiries
**Proof used:** Hawaii wedding — $35k budget, completely dry venue, hit budget through vendor negotiation

---

### Hook options

**Hook A (primary — real story opener):**
> "A couple came to me with a $35,000 budget for a Hawaii wedding. Everyone told them it couldn't be done. Here's what actually happened."

**Hook B (variation — tension first):**
> "The venue had nothing. No catering. No furniture. No bar. No florals. No lighting. Everything had to come in. Budget: $35k. Location: Hawaii."

**Hook C (experiment — result first):**
> "We planned a full Hawaii wedding for $35,000. It was beautiful. Here's how."

---

### Script

**HOOK**
> "A couple came to me with a $35,000 budget. For Hawaii. And a completely dry venue — meaning we had to bring in everything."

**RETAIN** *(the story, told in beats)*

> "The first thing I did was figure out where the money actually had to go versus where we could be creative."

> "A dry venue sounds like a nightmare. But it's actually an opportunity — because you're not paying for someone else's aesthetic. You're building your own from scratch."

> "We sourced everything locally on the island to cut freight costs. Every vendor we brought in, I negotiated. Not to be cheap — to protect the design budget."

> "The florals were the design anchor. We put real money there. Everything else — linens, furniture, lighting — we sourced with the design concept in mind, not the price tag."

> "The day came. Everything was in place. The couple walked in and cried."

> "We didn't go over budget. Not by a dollar."

**BRIDGE**
> "Here's what I learned: budget isn't the constraint people think it is. Taste and intention go further than money. What kills a wedding isn't a tight budget — it's not knowing what to prioritize."

**REWARD**
> "If you're planning a wedding and the budget feels impossible — it's not. It's a design problem, not a math problem. And design problems have solutions."

**CTA**
> *Caption:* "Every wedding I plan starts with a design conversation, not a budget conversation. Link in bio."

---

### Production notes
- TikTok: Talking head with photos from the Hawaii wedding overlaid during the story beats (if client approved for content use — confirm this)
- Instagram: Turn this into a carousel — slide 1 is the hook text, slides 2–6 are Hawaii wedding detail photos, final slide is the takeaway/reward
- If Hawaii photos aren't clearable for public use, run this as a pure story format (no photos) — the tension still works

## PIECE 03 — "My First Big Event Was in Oxford, England. I Planned It From San Diego."

**Format:** TikTok (story format) — this one is pure personality/brand building
**Platform priority:** TikTok first, can repurpose as IG Reel
**Drive type:** Desire (aspiration, trust-building, "this planner is serious")
**Metrics goal:** Watch time + Follows
**Proof used:** Oxford ball at the Divinity School — first large event, planned internationally

---

### Hook options

**Hook A (primary — specificity + scale):**
> "My first big event was a ball at the Divinity School in Oxford, England. I planned it from San Diego."

**Hook B (variation — fish-out-of-water):**
> "I got a call asking me to plan an event in Oxford, England. I said yes before I knew what I was getting into."

**Hook C (experiment — result-first):**
> "I've planned events in Hawaii and Oxford, England. The most important thing I learned wasn't about design."

---

### Script

**HOOK**
> "My first big event was a ball at the Divinity School in Oxford, England. I planned the whole thing from San Diego."

**RETAIN**

> "The Divinity School is one of the most beautiful buildings I've ever seen. It's been standing for 600 years. You don't transform a space like that — you honor it."

> "So my design approach was the opposite of what I usually do. Instead of building an environment, I was curating one. Every choice had to belong in that room — not compete with it."

> "Planning it from 5,000 miles away meant I couldn't physically touch anything until I landed. Every vendor call, every layout decision, every contingency — done remotely, across time zones."

> "I landed in Oxford two days before the event. And for the first time, I saw everything in person."

> "It was exactly what I'd designed."

**BRIDGE**
> "That event taught me that design isn't about being in the room. It's about having a clear enough vision that everyone around you can execute it — even when you're not there."

**REWARD**
> "That's what I bring to every event now. A vision clear enough to survive the distance between concept and execution. Whether I'm planning in San Diego, Hawaii, or somewhere in Europe — the process is the same."

**CTA**
> *Caption:* "I work with couples anywhere. If you're planning something that doesn't fit the standard template — let's talk. Link in bio."

---

### Production notes
- This is a pure talking-head storytelling piece — no footage required
- If you have photos from Oxford, use them as B-roll over the "retain" section
- The Oxford story is particularly powerful for signaling destination capability and design seriousness — use it early in the content calendar
- Tone should be personal and reflective, not braggy — the humility of "I said yes before I knew what I was getting into" is what makes it trustworthy

## PIECE 04 — "This Chair Decision Will Age Your Wedding Photos Faster Than Anything Else"

**Format:** Instagram Carousel (design education, high-save) + TikTok talking head (shortened)
**Platform priority:** Instagram carousel first (save driver), TikTok as derivative
**Drive type:** Fear (fear of a wedding that looks dated in 5 years)
**Metrics goal:** Saves
**Proof used:** Planner's design expertise and professional POV

---

### Hook options

**Carousel first-slide hook (bold statement format):**

**Option A:**
> "The chair decision that will date your wedding photos in five years."

**Option B:**
> "Your chair is in every single photo. Most couples don't think about it until it's too late."

**Option C:**
> "One decision determines how your wedding photos age. Most couples get it wrong."

---

### Carousel script (slide by slide)

**Slide 1 — Hook**
> "The chair decision that will date your wedding photos in five years."

**Slide 2 — The problem**
> "Your ceremony and reception chairs are in nearly every photo taken at your wedding. They're part of your backdrop, your aisle, your table — they're everywhere. And most couples choose them last."

**Slide 3 — What dates quickly**
> "The Chiavari chair had its moment. Gold Chiavari specifically. You know the one. If you've been to 10 weddings in the last decade, you've sat in it at 8 of them. It reads 'wedding rental catalog,' not 'event designed for us.'"

**Slide 4 — What photographs expensively**
> "The chairs that hold up over time tend to share a few qualities: they have visual weight (not spindle-thin legs), a material story (wood, velvet, rattan — something with texture), and they were chosen for the room they're in, not for the rental catalog they came from."

**Slide 5 — What to actually ask**
> "When you're selecting chairs, ask: does this chair belong in this room? Does the material make sense next to my linens and florals? Would I put this chair in a photo I want to print and hang? If you hesitate on any of those — keep looking."

**Slide 6 — The real point**
> "A chair isn't a chair. It's part of a designed environment. Everything in the room should be chosen, not defaulted."

**Slide 7 — Reward / payoff**
> "The couples who hire me never have to think about this. It's already decided — intentionally, specifically, for their event. That's what designing an event means."

**Slide 8 — CTA**
> "If you want every decision made with this level of intention — link in bio."

---

### TikTok derivative (condensed talking head version)

> **Hook:** "Your chair is in every photo at your wedding. And most couples choose it based on whatever the venue offers. Here's why that matters."

> **Retain:** Condense slides 3–5 into 30 seconds of direct spoken points

> **Bridge/Reward:** "A designed event means every single thing in the room was chosen. Nothing defaulted. That's the difference between decorated and designed."

---

### Production notes
- Carousel: Use real photos for slides 3–5 if possible (chair examples from past events or reference images)
- If no usable photos, text-only slides still work well with strong typography
- This format gets saved by couples who are actively in planning — high algorithm value
- The TikTok derivative should run 1–2 weeks after the carousel to reinforce the topic across platforms

## PIECE 05 — Mood Board → Oxford Ball Reveal (Concept to Execution)

**Format:** Instagram Carousel (concept-first, authority-building) + TikTok (reveal/BTS format)
**Platform priority:** Instagram carousel first, TikTok reveal second
**Drive type:** Desire (aspiration — couples want to see the thinking, not just the result)
**Metrics goal:** Saves + Profile visits + DM inquiries
**Proof used:** Oxford Divinity School ball — design concept, material choices, finished event photos

---

### Carousel hook options (first slide)

**Option A:**
> "The design brief for our Oxford ball. And what it looked like when we brought it to life."

**Option B:**
> "This is how I approach every event. The concept first. Everything else follows."

**Option C:**
> "Most planners start with a venue. I start with a feeling. Here's what that looks like in practice."

---

### Carousel script

**Slide 1 — Hook**
> "This is how I approach every event. The concept first. Everything else follows."

**Slide 2 — The brief / design intention**
> "The brief for our Oxford ball was simple: don't fight the room. The Divinity School has been standing for 600 years. Gothic arches, medieval stone, candlelight. It already had everything it needed — our job was to belong in it."

> "The design direction: warm, candlelit, archival. Like stepping into an oil painting."

**Slide 3 — Palette / material story**
> "We chose deep jewel tones — burgundy, forest, antique gold — that would deepen in candlelight rather than compete with it. The linens were textural, not shiny. The florals were loose, organic, almost wild — the kind that look like they grew there."

*(photo: mood board or palette reference)*

**Slide 4 — The detail logic**
> "Every material decision went through the same filter: does this belong in this room? Not 'is this beautiful' in the abstract — 'does this belong HERE?' That question cuts 80% of options immediately."

**Slide 5 — The challenge**
> "I planned it from San Diego. I'd never stood in the room. Every decision was made remotely, on photos and floor plans and trust in the vendors I'd spent weeks briefing."

**Slide 6 — The reveal**
> "I landed in Oxford two days before the event."

*(photo: venue/event photo — ideally the most striking angle)*

**Slide 7 — The result**
> "It was exactly what I'd designed."

*(photo: another event detail shot)*

**Slide 8 — The point**
> "This is what 'designed not decorated' means. It's not about a style or a trend. It's about having a concept clear enough that every single choice — the linens, the florals, the candlelight, the chair — belongs. Nothing is there by accident."

**Slide 9 — Reward**
> "When a space has that kind of intention, guests feel it. They can't name it. But they feel it."

**Slide 10 — CTA**
> "This is what I build for every couple I work with. If you want an event that feels like somewhere specific — link in bio."

---

### TikTok derivative (reveal format)

> **Hook:** "Watch what the Divinity School in Oxford looked like for our event." *(visual: exterior of building or empty interior)*

> **Retain:** Quick BTS or photo reel of the event set up — let the images do the work, light music

> **Bridge:** *(voiceover or text)* "I planned this from San Diego. Never stood in the room until two days before. This is what I'd designed."

> **Reveal:** Final event photo — best angle, most striking image

> **Reward:** *(text on screen)* "Designed. Not decorated."

> **CTA:** *(end card)* "Link in bio."

---

### Production notes
- The TikTok version can be a pure photo/video reel set to music (zero-text aspirational format) — this is the "beauty-forward" format that Instagram and TikTok favor for watch time
- The carousel is the authority-builder; the TikTok is the beauty signal — they serve different purposes
- If Oxford photos are limited, this piece can still work as a text/design process carousel with any strong event photo as the "reveal" slide
- "Designed. Not decorated." as a closing line is repeatable across pieces — consider making it your signature sign-off
